Feed Me

Donating excess meal credits

Role

Product Designer
Front-End Engineer

Duration

November 2019 - Ongoing

Team

Brandon Lee, Erica Li (Back-end Engineers)

Skills
Product Design Python Flask/CSS/JS Passion Project

Challenge

Helping college students profit from excess meal credits while creating societal impact.

Outcome

A mobile app that connects students with extra meal credits to those who want cheaper meals. Jump to the final designs!

Impact

Overview

At Brown University, both freshmen and sophomores are required to buy a meal plan, which has an excess of meal credits. Therefore, we want to connect upperclassmen looking for cheap meals with underclassmen who can't finish their swipes.

We began this project at HackPrinceton2019. I was responsible for research, design, and front-end development (HTML/CSS/JS, then integrated with Flask back-end). Afterward, I redesigned Feed Me for usability improvements.

Problem

How do we connect students with excess meal credits to those who need it, while incentivizing them to reach out to one another?

Research

To set the project off by defining the problem space, I collected data from 20+ recent articles and conducted 10+ semi-structured interviews with my friends (age 18-22) over messenger (as well as some Q&As with other hackers).

Personas

From my research and interviews, I synthesized 2 personas that represent our user group. This helps us better visualize their frustrations and ideate solutions.

Pain Points

  1. People off-meal plan think both the meal plan and takeout options are too expensive for college students.
  2. Upperclassmen don't have time to cook and miss the convenience of cafeteria food.
  3. Freshmen and sophomores wish they can get their money back for leftover swipes.
  4. Underclassmen who don't have upperclassmen friends don't know who needs swipes.

Ethics

Since our app deals directly with users' money, we want them to feel safe when using Feed Me. Therefore, we brainstormed solutions to ensure user privacy and security that is within our technical capabilities.

Not everything is about money.

Beyond incentivizing students with monetary rewards, we want to empower them to help those who are food insecure at school and in the greater Rhode Island community. Thus, I proposed that Givers can provide free meals for less financially privileged students. Moreover, we decided to automatically donate $2 for every $5 to RI food pantries.

Solution

Consequently, Feed Me allows Buyers to purchase a meal swipe from a Giver for a default $5. This is a lot less than $13, the average cost of a meal at an inexpensive restaurant in Rhode Island (Numbeo). For each meal, the Giver earns $3 and donates $2 to the Rhode Island Community Food Pantry. Givers can also provide a free meal for less financially privileged students.

Project Goals

  1. Create a safe and easy way to share and claim meals
  2. Incentivize Givers to share through monetary reward and social impact
  3. Support students who face financial concerns and food insecurity
  4. Positively impact the greater local community

Ideation

Competitive Analysis

We compared Feed Me to similar initiatives and the main differences we noticed were:

  • Business Model: Dependent on student's altruism to donate free meals vs. partial financial reward
  • Student Interaction: Donate extra swipes to a fund vs. meeting the student you are helping
  • Social Impact: Programs that train students for social entrepreneurship vs. direct donations to charity

User Flow

Keeping the product requirements in mind, I constructed a basic flow of sharing and claiming a meal. This helps us see how the user navigates the web app based on user goals.

Wireframing and Testing

Initial Design

After several rounds of wireframe critiques, I moved on to mocking up the high-fidelity flow of our prototype 1.0. The motivation behind the visual design was to represent the students' collaboration with a warm gradient and associate the app with an appetizing feeling.

Redesign

However, Feed Me was done in a 3-day time crunch, so I took the initiative to redesign its experience and branding. In the new design, when a meal is claimed, the interface transitions from cool to warm colors to represent the growing warmth of eating and joy in helping others. I also focused on constructing a clear navigation and visual hierarchy.

Final Design

After a long journey, Feed Me is revamped!

Decision 1

Quick Sharing

Only 3 steps to share a meal. Givers can simply go about their lunch schedule until the Buyer calls them.

Decision 2

Simple and Secure

Users are automatically registered with their Brown email addresses and can pay with their Bear Bucks. No bank cards are required!

Decision 3

Making a social impact

For every $5 meal bought, the Feeder earns $3 and donates $2 to the RI Community Food Pantry. Win-win for the student and the local community.

Decision 4

All-inclusive

The Giver can choose to provide a free meal for less financially privileged students. The meal‚Äôs full $5 cost will be donated to charity if bought by a regular student. Win-win-win for the student, RI residents, and the school community!

Impact

Despite facing many technical challenges, my teammates and I felt a great sense of pride and accomplishment in our ability to create a program that people could use in only three days.

What's Next?

We are currently working on prototype 2.0, aiming to launch our app in 2021.

For future design improvements, I would look into:

  • Interactive onboarding experience
  • Achievement badges to create more incentive and fun
  • Testing, testing, and more testing!

I also learned a lot working in a team with SWEs:

  • Set the scope of the project! Focus on the essential 1-2 functionalities and use research to support why those are the priorities. Use a Product Requirement Description so everyone is on the same page.
  • Holistic experience over aesthetics. All the time!
