412 Food Rescue

Redistrubuting surplus food

UX Designer & Researcher
November 2019, 2 weeks
Lucy Qu, Jenny He, Milanca Wang
UI/UX Design Interaction Design Social Good

Overview

This project was created for the course User Interfaces and User Experience at Brown University taught by Jeff Huang.

Our goal was to design an app for a startup based on just their description. We chose 412 Food Rescue, who partners with food retailers to pick up their healthy surplus food and deliver it to community nonprofit organizations visited by people facing food insecurity.

    Check out the Figma interactive prototype!

Ethical Impact

Before starting the design process, we considered how our app might affect various groups of people.

Problem

How do we efficiently connect food providers, volunteers, and community NGOs to ethically reduce food waste?

Solution

We created a unified platform for picking up and delivering food. Suppliers begin by posting pickups. Then, a volunteer can choose from all the posted pickups and the app will calculate the most efficient route to the community NGO. Lastly, the NGO will be notified when a volunteer is delivering food to them.

User Research

To begin, we focused our research on the supplier, volunteer, and NGO by synthesizing information from participants' comments about 412 Food Rescue on their official Facebook page.

Key Insights

The key insights align with my initial hypothesis:

The Supplier focuses more on business goals, while the Volunteer and NGO are propelled by social good.

User Flow

Next, we mapped out the user journey flow of three user groups - food suppliers, volunteers, and community NGOs.

Process and Iterations

With the above user flow, we created wireframes for each user group. Then, we conducted brief user testing on our friends by assigning each of them a user group. Their comments are noted below each wireframe.

After the 1st Iteration…

Based on the feedback, we added new screens and integrated them into a wireflow. Some notable changes include:

  • Delivery Details page lists information such as trip distance, food to pick up, pickup time frame
  • Pickup/Drop-off Confirmations that have more personalized messages when the volunteer arrives
  • A congratulatory message at the end of a delivery

After the 2nd Iteration…

Based on our new wireframes, we created hi-fi prototypes and presented the volunteer screens to our class of 20 students (in-class critique only allowed one user flow submission).

Style Guide

Our main color is green, which represents the environmental and social impact our app creates.

Hi-Fi Prototypes

After three iterations, we finalized the designs for the three user flows and they are displayed below.

    Check out the Figma interactive prototype!

1. Supplier

The supplier can post a task, wait for a volunteer to claim it, then confirm the pickup. Their accomplishments are also accessed on the dashboard.

2. Volunteer

The volunteer can pick a task from the dashboard, and the app will guide them to the pickup and drop-off locations. They can also view their achievements by clicking into their Profile.

3. NGO

The NGO can enable food deliveries on the home page. Then, they can see volunteer details at the top and confirm their delivery.

User Testing

Lastly, we submitted our volunteer hi-fi prototype to UserTesting.com.

Our hypothesis is that the one-directional design of this app makes navigation user-friendly and allows users to complete the tasks with few distractions. Three UX researchers (alias Abby, Bryan, Caleb) tested our interface, and their responses and quantitatively and qualitatively analyzed below.

User Satisfaction

Our low time on task averages support that the users are generally satisfied by the app’s simplicity, with Caleb saying “Nothing is confusing.” Abby mentioned that seeing her achievements at the end of delivery makes her feel an “adrenaline rush,” and Beth noted that her accomplishment metrics keep her engaged.

Conclusion

Overall, our hypothesis was supported because our users were able to successfully use the app to make a delivery in 2:45 minutes. Therefore, we accept our hypothesis because users were able to complete a delivery without getting distracted in the process.

What's Next?

For future UX improvements, I would look into:

1. Accessibility for Disabilities Improvements

  • For supplier/NGO contacts with visual impairments, include a note that tells the volunteer to call the supplier/NGO employer rather than text.
  • For volunteers with hearing impairments, include a note that tells the supplier/NGO employer to text rather than call.

2. Achievements panel to increase motivation

  • Make Achievements a plant that grows with donations, so suppliers and volunteers can visually see their contributions in helping the planet go green.

3. Onboarding pages for elderly NGO workers

  • Focusing on teaching simple navigations for pickups.
